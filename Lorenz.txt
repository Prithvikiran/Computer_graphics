What is the Lorenz Attractor?

The Lorenz attractor is a set of chaotic solutions to a system of three
differential equations originally derived by meteorologist Edward Lorenz
in 1963.

He was trying to model atmospheric convection — specifically, how a thin
layer of fluid behaves when heated from below and cooled from above
(like the Earth’s atmosphere).

What he discovered accidentally was something profound: a deterministic
system (no randomness whatsoever) that produces completely unpredictable
long-term behavior. This was one of the founding discoveries of chaos
theory.

The Equations

dx/dt = σ(y − x)

dy/dt = x(ρ − z) − y

dz/dt = xy − βz

Where: - x = convection rate - y = horizontal temperature variation - z
= vertical temperature variation

The Three Parameters

1.  Sigma (σ) — Prandtl Number Controls the ratio of fluid viscosity to
    thermal diffusivity. Think of it as how quickly momentum diffuses
    compared to heat.

At σ = 10, momentum spreads 10× faster than temperature through the
fluid.

Higher sigma = more viscous behavior relative to heat conduction.

2.  Rho (ρ) — Rayleigh Number The most important parameter.

Represents the temperature difference driving convection. It is the
bifurcation parameter — the value where the system’s behavior
fundamentally changes.

-   ρ < 1: Fluid stays still, no convection
-   1 < ρ < ~24.74: Stable convection rolls form
-   ρ > ~24.74: Chaos emerges and the famous butterfly attractor appears

3.  Beta (β) — Geometric Factor Related to the physical shape of the
    convective rolls, specifically the aspect ratio of the fluid layer.

It appears in the z-equation and damps the vertical temperature
variation.

The commonly used value 8/3 comes directly from the geometry of Lorenz’s
simplified fluid model.

Properties of the Attractor

Deterministic Chaos The equations contain no randomness. Given identical
starting conditions, the system always produces the same trajectory.

However, two trajectories starting infinitesimally close together
diverge exponentially fast. This phenomenon is known as the butterfly
effect.

Strange Attractor The trajectory never repeats, never crosses itself,
and yet remains bounded within the butterfly-shaped region.

It is neither periodic nor random. Instead, it forms a fractal structure
with a non-integer dimension of approximately 2.06.

Sensitivity to Initial Conditions Tiny changes in x, y, or z at time t =
0 eventually produce dramatically different trajectories.

This is one of the reasons weather forecasting becomes unreliable beyond
roughly two weeks.

Two Lobes The trajectory spirals around one wing for a while and then
unpredictably switches to the other.

The number of loops around each wing before switching has no repeating
pattern and is chaotic.

Real-Life Applications

Meteorology - Foundation of chaos theory - Explains limits of long-range
weather forecasting

Engineering - Secure communications - Chaotic signals can mask and
encrypt data transmissions

Biology - Neural firing patterns - Cardiac arrhythmias - Population
dynamics

Economics - Chaotic models of markets and financial instability

Cryptography - Chaos-based random number generators

Fluid Dynamics - Turbulence modeling - Pipe flow and aerodynamics

View Angles

Azimuth (th) Horizontal rotation, similar to rotating a globe left or
right.

In a visualization program, arrow keys often modify this value to orbit
around the attractor.

Elevation (ph) Vertical tilt, similar to viewing the attractor from
above (ph = 90°) or from the side (ph = 0°).

A starting value around ph = 15° provides a slightly elevated view that
clearly shows both wings.

Together, azimuth and elevation allow free navigation around the 3D
butterfly shape and help reveal its structure from different
perspectives.
